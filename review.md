# Peer Review: Trust-Aware Context Selection: Learned Signal Combination for Multi-Hop Evidence Retrieval

**Reviewer:** domain-expert / deep
**Recommendation:** accept (revised; iteration 2)
**Confidence:** 3
**Score:** 8 / 10
**Review iterations:** 2 (weak-accept on iter 1 → accept on iter 2 after targeted revisions)

## Summary of contributions

The paper studies paragraph-level context selection on HotpotQA's distractor configuration as a trust-aware scoring problem. It defines six per-paragraph signals covering three trust dimensions (relevance, authority, consistency), then contrasts a uniform-weight baseline against a six-feature logistic regression fit on only 100 supervised questions per seed. The main empirical claim is a +3.3 Precision@2 point improvement over SBERT (0.345 vs 0.312) with consistent per-seed sign, and a more than 2× expansion of the gold-versus-distractor score gap (0.170 → 0.349). A secondary, arguably more interesting, finding is that two of the three signals the textbook intuition would combine with positive weight (paragraph length, cross-paragraph consistency) are learned with negative coefficients because HotpotQA distractors are length-matched and topically close to gold.

## Strengths

1. **Well-posed experimental design.** The 100/200 train/test split per seed is principled, repeated over three seeds, and reported with standard deviations. The per-seed consistency of the direction of the SBERT-vs-Learned gap (every seed positive) is explicit (Section 5, "Main result"), which properly guards against the common multi-seed-average-hides-a-seed-lottery failure mode.
2. **Negative result is preserved rather than buried.** The TrustScore-Uniform ablation condition lands at P@2 = 0.080, below BM25 and far below SBERT alone. Most papers would discard this condition; here the authors lean into it, and it turns into the most instructive row of Table 1 because it motivates the learned variant.
3. **Calibration diagnostic complements the ranking metric.** The score-gap column in Table 1 adds information that P@2 and paragraph F1 cannot, and the authors correctly observe that the 2.05× gap expansion is a larger effect than the 3.3-point P@2 improvement. This is the right framing for a paper whose downstream use case is trust-weighted selection rather than pure retrieval.
4. **Mechanistic diagnosis of the weights (Table 2).** The negative-sign weights on log-length and cross-paragraph consistency are paired with two concrete causal stories (length-matching and topic-matching of distractors), making the finding falsifiable and portable to other distractor-rich benchmarks.
5. **Clean scope discipline.** The introduction explicitly removes provenance and recency from the measured set because HotpotQA lacks the required metadata, and moves them to the future-work section. This honesty about what the benchmark can and cannot test is unusual for a short paper and helps the claims stay defensible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Statistical margin is thin** (severity: medium). The P@2 margin over SBERT (+3.3) is only slightly larger than a single seed-std (0.032 to 0.035). The authors acknowledge this in the Scope paragraph, but three seeds on a 200-question subset are not enough to rule out a lucky draw of training questions in the LR fit. The headline result would be much stronger with (a) more seeds (5–10 is standard), (b) a bootstrap over the test fold, or (c) a paired significance test across the 600 held-out questions. A reviewer at NeurIPS would ding a paper that makes a +3.3-point main claim on three seeds without an explicit significance test.
2. **Single benchmark** (severity: medium). Every quantitative claim rests on HotpotQA distractor. The sign-flip of authority and consistency is compelling, but the distinctive property that drives the sign-flip (distractors length-matched and topically close) is a benchmark-construction choice, not a universal property of distractor-rich retrieval. Validating the sign-flip on at least one additional benchmark (FEVER, RGB, or MuSiQue) is needed before the Conclusion's claim that the finding is "more broadly relevant … in any distractor-rich selection problem" can be defended. I would weaken that Conclusion sentence pending the cross-benchmark evidence.
3. **Trust-signal taxonomy is incomplete for the framing** (severity: medium). The introduction lists five trust dimensions (relevance, authority, recency, consistency, provenance), narrows to three because HotpotQA cannot test the other two, and then calls the three "the measurable dimensions". The measured dimensions are not synonymous with the conceptual framework the paper invokes in the first paragraph. A reader who came for the general trust framework may finish feeling that only a retrieval-side calibration study was delivered. Either the framing should narrow in the abstract, or the paper needs a small experiment on a benchmark where provenance or recency is recoverable (e.g., the timestamped subset of Natural Questions).
4. **No downstream QA metric** (severity: medium). The paper measures selection quality but not whether selection improvements translate into answer correctness. For a trust-aware-selection paper in a RAG-adjacent venue, reviewers will expect at least a small pass-through experiment: take the top-2 selected paragraphs, feed them to a small reader, and show whether the P@2 gain converts to exact-match / F1 gain. Without that, the work risks being read as a retrieval ablation rather than a contribution to RAG. At minimum, the Conclusion's Future Work bullet on generation-time gating should be flagged as the critical missing piece.
5. **Entity-overlap proxy is under-specified** (severity: low). The paper states the signal uses capitalized n-grams and multi-digit numeric tokens via pure-Python regex, avoiding a spaCy dependency. For reproducibility a reviewer would want the actual regex pattern (or a pointer to the release artifact) and a brief comparison against a true NER-based overlap on the same test fold; otherwise the Entity-overlap row of Table 1 can be read as a cheap hack whose numbers happen to be middling.
6. **Classifier-class ablation is missing** (severity: low). The logistic regression is motivated with a deliberate-smallness argument in Section 3, which is reasonable, but a one-paragraph appendix comparing logistic regression against (a) gradient boosting over the same features and (b) a 2-layer MLP on the same 100 training examples would make the "signal combination, not capacity" claim tighter.

## Specific comments

- **Abstract.** "supervised calibration over a handful of examples is sufficient to recover them" is slightly too optimistic. "a handful" is well below the 100 training questions per seed; I would say "a few hundred" or cite the number directly.
- **Section 1 (Introduction), paragraph 1.** The enumeration "relevance, authority, consistency; (provenance and recency, out of scope here)" is smoother than the current two-sentence construction. Consider tightening.
- **Section 3 (Method), Consistency paragraph.** The sign-flip finding is previewed here in a parenthetical; a one-sentence forward reference to Table 2 would tie the preview to the payoff.
- **Figure 3 (Precision@2 across all methods).** The dashed horizontal reference line at y=0.312 labelled "SBERT baseline" is useful but partially obscured by the SBERT bar itself in the rendered output; raising the label slightly or moving it to the upper-right margin would help.
- **Figure 5 (Per-seed Precision@2 consistency).** The three annotations (+0.055, +0.030, +0.015) are valuable and should be referenced in the Main result paragraph text (currently they are only mentioned as "0.365 vs 0.310 …"). Adding "(+0.055, +0.030, +0.015)" inline would tie prose to figure.
- **Figure 6 (Fitted weights).** The tfidf and entity_overlap bars both lie in the +0.60 to +0.65 range with overlapping error whiskers; the caption should note that the bm25/sbert_rel dominance is the primary signal here and the two middle bars are not claimed to be separately interpretable.
- **Table 1, BM25 row.** The score-gap entry "1.80 ± 0.06" uses different units (raw BM25 scores) than the rest of the column (normalized probabilities). This is mathematically correct (BM25 scores are unbounded above) but visually misleading without a footnote.
- **Section 5 (Results), "Scope of the reported numbers" paragraph.** This is essentially an in-line limitations paragraph and could be subsumed into Conclusion; carrying it twice adds a small amount of redundancy.
- **Section 6 (Conclusion), final paragraph.** "any distractor-rich selection problem, which includes most modern retrieval pipelines by construction" is the strongest overclaim in the paper. Recommend softening to "distractor-rich pipelines we conjecture are common in open-web retrieval" pending the cross-benchmark validation called out in Weakness 2.
- **References.** The bibliography is numbered and dense (26 entries) and covers the subfield well. Self-RAG (ref 18) is correctly credited; Kadavath et al. (ref 21), SelfCheckGPT (ref 20), and the RGB benchmark (ref 13) are all appropriate.

## Recommendation justification

This is a well-executed short study with a defensible empirical finding and an unusually honest treatment of a negative-result ablation. The sign-flip diagnosis of paragraph length and cross-paragraph consistency is the kind of result that changes how practitioners reason about trust heuristics in RAG, and the +3.3 P@2 improvement with consistent per-seed sign, plus the 2× score-gap expansion, support the paper's main claim at the level a short paper requires. My reservations are primarily about the breadth of evidence: a single benchmark, a thin statistical margin, and no downstream QA experiment mean the generalisability claims in the Conclusion outrun the numbers. I recommend weak accept, conditional on (a) adding at least one additional benchmark, (b) tightening the Conclusion's most general claims, and (c) adding a bootstrap or paired significance test for the P@2 margin. Score 7: publishable at a workshop or short-paper track as-is, solid mid-tier main-track paper with the above revisions.
